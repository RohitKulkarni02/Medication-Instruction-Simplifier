Project Title: Lost in Simplification: Detecting Safety-Critical
Errors in LLM-Generated Medication Instructions

Section 1: Introduction & Motivation

Medication non-adherence occurs when a patient does not take their medication as prescribed. This affects up to 50% of patients with chronic diseases. This is known to contribute to at least 100,000 preventable deaths and $100 billion in avoidable healthcare costs per year. One driver of this issue is the complexity of drug labelling and instructions. The FDA mandates instructions for OTC medications, such as ibuprofen, all the way to schedule II prescriptions, such as oxycodone, to be written for clinical and regulatory audiences. This leaves patients confused with the dense language. LLMs offer a compelling solution to this issue: they are able to rewrite complex medical text into easy to digest, patient-friendly language in an instant. Simplification poses a major risk, though. If the model lessens the urgency of a contraindication, gives an incorrect dose constraint, or completely omits a black-box warning, the simplified output can directly endanger a patient’s health, including a risk of life threatening injuries or death. Our project investigates this issue by leveraging generative AI to improve medication comprehension while systematically detecting the critical safety errors that simplification can introduce. We chose this problem because there is a high-impact use case with a measurable and important AI risk to evaluate, and the openFDA API gives us structured absolute truth to evaluate against.


Section 2: Research Question / Problem Statement
Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong capabilities in summarizing and simplifying complex documents. In healthcare, these capabilities could help translate technical medication instructions into language that is easier for patients to understand. Medication package inserts provided by regulatory agencies such as the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) contain critical information about dosage, contraindications, drug interactions, and safety warnings, but they are often written for clinicians rather than patients. Generative AI systems have the potential to improve accessibility by simplifying these instructions into more readable formats.
However, applying generative models in safety-critical domains introduces significant risks. During simplification, LLMs may unintentionally omit, alter, or weaken important safety information. Missing dosage instructions, dropped warnings, or incomplete contraindications could lead to incorrect medication usage and reduced patient safety. Understanding these potential failure modes is essential before generative AI systems can be safely applied in healthcare communication.
The central research question of this project is:
Do LLM-based simplifications of medication package inserts introduce systematic errors in adherence-critical sections such as dosing instructions, warnings, contraindications, and drug interactions?
In addition to this primary question, our project will investigate several related questions:
How frequently do simplified instructions omit or alter safety-critical information?
Are certain sections (e.g., warnings or drug interactions) more likely to be lost during simplification?
Do error patterns vary across different medication categories such as antibiotics, cardiovascular drugs, or psychiatric medications?
What trade-offs exist between improved readability and preservation of safety-critical information?
To answer these questions, we will evaluate simplified outputs using quantitative metrics including error rates in adherence-critical sections and standard readability measures. By systematically analyzing these outcomes across different generative models, this project aims to better understand the reliability and limitations of LLM-based simplification in safety-sensitive healthcare applications.


Section 3: Scope of the Project
What we will explore:
Our project focuses on evaluating the reliability of LLM-generated simplifications of FDA medication package inserts, specifically targeting the sections most critical to patient safety and medication adherence. Within our four-week timeframe and three-person team, we will concentrate on the following:
We will build an end-to-end pipeline that ingests structured drug labels from the openFDA API, generates plain-language simplifications using GPT-4o and LLaMA 3, and then systematically audits the output for adherence-critical errors. Our error detection will focus on four specific categories: dropped warnings, softened contraindications, incorrect dosing frequency, and missing drug-food/drug-drug interactions. We will evaluate error rates across three representative drug categories: antibiotics, cardiac medications, and psychiatric medications chosen because they vary in dosing complexity, interaction severity, and patient population. Finally, we will analyze the tradeoff between readability improvement and error introduction, and compare performance across model sizes to determine whether larger models produce fewer safety-critical errors.
What we will not address:
We will not attempt to build a deployable patient-facing tool or conduct any user studies with real patients or clinicians. Our evaluation relies on automated comparison against original FDA labels and LLM-as-judge methods rather than expert pharmacological review. We will not cover all drug categories available through openFDA; instead, we limit our analysis to three categories to keep the comparison tractable. We also will not explore fine-tuning or RAG-based approaches for simplification; our focus is on evaluating zero-shot and few-shot prompting behavior, not on optimizing simplification quality. Lastly, we will not address multilingual simplification or health literacy variations across patient demographics.

Section 4: Plan of Action
To investigate our research question, we will develop a structured pipeline that generates simplified medication instructions using generative models and automatically evaluates the safety and completeness of those simplifications. The project will follow a four-week development and evaluation timeline.
Week 1: Data Collection and Preparation
We will collect medication package insert data from publicly available sources such as the openFDA Drug Label API and the DailyMed database. These datasets contain structured pharmaceutical documentation including dosage instructions, contraindications, warnings, and drug interactions. During this phase, we will design a data ingestion pipeline to retrieve and preprocess the drug label data. We will also identify and extract adherence-critical sections from the original labels to serve as the ground truth for later comparisons.
Week 2: LLM-Based Simplification Pipeline
In the second phase, we will develop a pipeline that uses generative AI models to produce simplified, patient-friendly medication instructions from the original drug labels. Different prompting strategies will be explored to generate concise and readable instructions while attempting to preserve safety-critical information. Simplified outputs will be stored alongside the original drug labels to enable systematic analysis.
Week 3: Safety Section Extraction and Error Detection
We will implement an automated analysis system to extract adherence-critical sections such as dosage instructions, contraindications, warnings, and drug interactions from both the original drug labels and the simplified outputs. Using these extracted sections, we will compare simplified instructions against the original labels to detect missing, altered, or weakened safety information. Identified discrepancies will be categorized into error types such as missing warnings, incorrect dosage instructions, softened contraindications, or missing drug interaction information.
Week 4: Evaluation and Comparative Analysis
In the final phase, we will conduct a comprehensive evaluation of the simplification pipeline. We will measure the frequency and types of errors introduced during simplification and analyze how these errors vary across medication categories. Additionally, we will evaluate improvements in readability using standard readability metrics. Where possible, we will compare the performance of different generative models to identify differences in reliability and error patterns. The results will be summarized using tables, visualizations, and statistical analysis to highlight key insights regarding the use of generative AI for simplifying safety-critical medical documents.

Section 5: Data & Resources
Data Sources:
Our primary data source is the openFDA Drug Label API (api.fda.gov), a fully public REST API that provides structured access to FDA-approved medication package inserts. Each label includes machine-readable fields for dosage and administration, warnings, contraindications, drug interactions, and adverse reactions, which serve as our ground truth for evaluating simplification accuracy. We will supplement this with DailyMed (dailymed.nlm.nih.gov), the NIH's drug label repository, which provides labels in Structured Product Labeling (SPL) XML format, offering finer-grained section parsing when the openFDA response lacks sufficient structure. Neither source requires authentication or institutional access. We plan to sample approximately 30–50 drug labels across our three target categories (antibiotics, cardiac medications, and psychiatric medications), balancing coverage with what is feasible to evaluate thoroughly in four weeks.
Models:
We will use GPT-4o and LLaMA 3 as our simplification models, prompting them in zero-shot and few-shot settings to generate plain-language patient instructions from the original label text. For adherence-critical error detection, we will employ GPT-4o as a judge, prompting it to compare the simplified output against the original label and flag discrepancies in dosing, warnings, contraindications, and interactions. We will also use SciBERT for medical named entity extraction to support structured comparison between source labels and simplified outputs.
Tools & Evaluation Resources:
For readability measurement, we will use standard metrics including the Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level and SMOG Index, available through Python libraries such as textstat. Our pipeline will be implemented in Python, using the requests library for API ingestion, xml.etree for SPL parsing, and the OpenAI and Hugging Face APIs for model inference. All code and results will be managed via GitHub.
